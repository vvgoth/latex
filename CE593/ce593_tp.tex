\section{Introduction}

\hspace{0.5cm}Wave hindcasting is a fundamental methodology in coastal and ocean engineering, serving as a "bridge" to the past when long-term, high-quality measured data is unavailable.By utilizing historical atmospheric records—specifically wind speed, duration, and fetch, engineers can reconstruct past sea states with precision. This process is indispensable for developing a comprehensive understanding of a specific site's wave climate over several decades.The process typically involves feeding historical wind fields into numerical wave models to generate time-series data for primary parameters, such as: 

\begin{itemize}
  \item Significant Wave Height ($H_s$): The average height of the highest one-third of waves.
  \item Peak Wave Period ($T_p$): The wave period associated with the most energetic waves in the total spectrum.
  \item Wave Direction: Essential for understanding sediment transport and shoreline morphology.
\end{itemize}

The main goal of this study is to recreate continuous wave conditions over time using numerical hindcasting based on wind data. This approach has two purposes: to describe the wave climate of the study area and to provide a reliable basis for validating future models.In contemporary coastal engineering literature, wave hindcasting is generally approached through two primary methodologies:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Physics-Based Spectral Models: These models (such as SWAN or WAVEWATCH III) solve the action balance equation to simulate wave energy propagation. While highly accurate, they are computationally intensive.
  \item Parametric Wind-Wave Formulations: These empirical methods relate wave parameters—specifically Significant Wave Height ($H_s$) and Peak Period ($T_p$)—directly to wind speed ($U$), fetch ($F$), and duration ($t$).

\end{itemize}

For long-term engineering applications and large-scale time-series analysis, parametric methods are often preferred. They offer a highly efficient and practical solution, allowing for the processing of decades of data without the prohibitive computational costs associated with full spectral modeling.

\section{Methodology}

\hspace{0.5cm}

Based on an extensive literature review and the specific operational scope of this research, the framework established by \citeA{Kamphuis2000WaveHindcasting} has been adopted. This methodology is renowned for its reliability in predicting wave growth in fetch-limited and duration-limited environments.\\

The implementation details of this study include:\\

Model Core: A continuous JONSWAP (Joint North Sea Wave Project) hindcast algorithm, which accounts for the non-linear transfer of energy and the peakedness of the wave spectrum.\\

Atmospheric Forcing: The model is driven by ERA5 wind reanalysis data, providing high-resolution global atmospheric parameters.\\

Validation Protocol: To ensure the accuracy of the hindcast, the output is rigorously validated against two independent benchmarks: the ERA5 wave reanalysis and the Copernicus Marine Service (CMEMS) datasets. This multi-source validation ensures the model's reliability across varying sea states and seasonal cycles.\\

Through this approach, the study transforms historical wind fields into a continuous, high-fidelity record of the wave environment, essential for coastal design and risk management.\\

 The procedure for determining significant wave height ($H_{mo}$) and peak wave period ($T_p$) for a single set of input conditions.\\

\begin{itemize}
  \item Inputs: The process begins by entering the wind speed ($U$), fetch length ($F$), and duration ($t$)
  \item Initial Computation: These values are used to calculate the dimensionless fetch ($F^*$) and dimensionless duration ($t^*$) using Eq. 5.3
  \item Parallel Paths: The methodology evaluates two possible sea states simultaneously:Fully Developed Sea (Path A): This assumes infinite fetch and duration, setting upper limits for non-dimensional height and period at $H_{mo}^* = 0.243$ and $T_p^* = 8.13$5555.Limited Sea State (Path B): This calculates an effective non-dimensional fetch ($F_{eff}^*$) based on the duration using Eq. 5.7
  \item Limiting Factor Determination: Within Path B, the model compares $F_{eff}^*$ and $F^*$.If $F_{eff}^* < F^*$, the waves are duration limited, and $F_{eff}^*$ is used in the growth equations.If $F_{eff}^* \ge F^*$, the waves are fetch limited, and the actual $F^*$ is used.
  \item Final Output: The dimensional wave parameters $H_{mo}$ and $T_p$ are calculated for both paths, and the final result is the minimum value between the fully developed and limited scenarios.
\end{itemize}

Jonswap Hindcasting for a Series of Input Conditions  extends the basic procedure into a loop to handle changing wind conditions over a series of time steps ($j$), such as hourly storm segments.

\begin{itemize}
  \item Inputs: Sequential Processing: For the initial time step ($j=1$), the model enters $U_1, t_1,$ and $F$ to run the basic hindcasting procedure
  \item Energy Carry-over: If the storm continues ($j < j_{max}$), the model accounts for the wave energy already generated.
  \item Virtual Duration Calculation: To transition between different wind speeds, the model calculates how long the new wind speed ($U_{j+1}$) would have needed to blow to create the existing wave height ($H_{mo,j}$)
  \begin{itemize}
    \item It first finds a new non-dimensional height: $H_{mo, j+1}^* = \frac{gH_{mo,j}}{U_{j+1}^2}$.
    \item It then computes a non-dimensional "virtual duration" $(t_{j+1}^{\prime})^{*}$ using the formula $(t_{j+1}^{\prime})^{*}=368000(H_{mo,j+1}^{*})^{4/3}$

  \end{itemize}
  \item Effective Duration: The physical virtual duration $t_{j+1}^{\prime}$ is determined and added to the actual duration of the next segment $(t_{j+1})$ to establish an effective duration $(t_{j+1,eff})$
  \item Looping: This effective duration and the new wind speed are used to compute the next state  ($F^*_{j+1}$ and $t^*_{j+1,eff}$), and the cycle repeats until the maximum number of segments is reached.
\end{itemize}
